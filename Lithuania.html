Language

Lithuanian Language:
Baltic family, closest to Latvian
Unique letters:
Ą, Č, Ę, Ė, Į, Š, Ų, Ū, Ž

Example sentence:

"Vieną vasaros rytą vaikščiojau parke ir pamačiau voverę, valgiančią mėlynes. Oras kvėpavo šviežiai, o saulė šildė mano veidą. Pamaniau, kad Suomijos gamta yra graži visuose metų laikuose."

("On a summer morning, I walked in the park and saw a squirrel eating blueberries. The air smelled fresh, and the sunshine warmed my face. I thought that Finnish nature is beautiful in all seasons.")

Landscape & Infrastructure

Traditional Lithuanian houses often combine wooden structures with white brick, featuring steep corrugated metal roofs. Modern variations include:

  • Pastel-colored stone walls
  • Mixed-material designs (wood + white brick)


Lithuanian Bollards:

  • Shape: Wedge-shaped (some thinner variants exist)
  • Reflectors:
    • Front: Orange (rectangular)
    • Back: White (rectangular)

Lithuanian Utility Poles

Structure:
Typically square concrete construction
Often feature a diagonal support beam
Poletop Characteristics:
Common Type:
Short horizontal rods holding insulators
Variant Type:
"Trident" insulator arrangement:
• 1 top insulator
• 2 side insulators (forming a Y-shape)

Note: These designs are shared with
other post-Soviet countries.

Lithuanian Road Markers
Placement: Positioned at a 45° angle toward the road.
Design: Distinctive L-shaped sign.

Baltic Kilometer Markers (All Countries):
Same blue design but installed at different angles:
∙ Lithuania: 45° angle towards the road (L-shape.)

Road Signs & Lanes

Lithuanian Pedestrian Signs:

  • Stripes: 3 (vs. Latvia’s 5).
  • Design: Simplified pedestrian silhouette (low detail).

Russia and Ukraine share the same design

Lithuanian Road Signs:

  • Thin red border + thin white outline
  • "ZONA" may appear below certain signs (e.g., speed limits), indicating a zone-based regulation

Lithuanian Directional Signs:
Blue background with white text
