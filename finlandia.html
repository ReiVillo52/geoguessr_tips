License Plates

Finland uses short white license plates with the standard EU blue stripe (with "FIN") on the left.

Language

Finnish stands apart from most European languages. Characterized by lengthy compound words and distinctive double vowels (e.g., "tie" [road] vs. "tiee" [roads']), it uses the special letters ä and ö. Example: "Helsingin yliopisto" (University of Helsinki).

Example sentence:

"Kesäaamuna kävelin puistossa ja näin oravan syömässä mustikoita. Ilma tuoksui raikkaalta, ja auringonpaiste lämmitti kasvojani. Ajattelin, että Suomen luonto on kaunis kaikkina vuodenaikoina."

("On a summer morning, I walked in the park and saw a squirrel eating blueberries. The air smelled fresh, and the sunshine warmed my face. I thought that Finnish nature is beautiful in all seasons.")

Landscape & Infrastructure

Outer road edge lines are consistently marked with solid white lines.

This differs from neighboring Sweden and Norway, which use dashed outer lines.


Finland has 3 types of line markings:

  • Standard white dashed lines (may be accompanied by an adjacent solid white line)
  • Combination lines: solid yellow with white dashes
  • Double yellow lines

Sweden never uses yellow middle lines. Norway uses distinctly orange-tinted center lines.

Finland uses distinctive black-and-white bollards with a long design, featuring a rectangular white reflector on the front and two small reflective dots on the back. The bollards come in two main variants: thicker round poles or thinner curved ones. The black stripes are typically painted in a diagonal pattern across the white base.

Finnish Architecture:
Urban: Modern concrete apartments
Suburban: Freestanding wooden homes
Traditional: Dark red wooden buildings (like Sweden/Norway)

Finnish Supermarkets:
K-Market (also called K-Supermarket/K-Citymarket) and S-Market operate only in Finland with distinct logos.

Lakes are everywhere — you're never far from water (Finland – "Land of a Thousand Lakes").

Road Signs & Lanes

Finland uses yellow as the standard background color for road signs.

Yellow road signs: Sweden & Iceland. White signs: Norway.

Red-bordered traffic signs in Finland have yellow edges — Unique to Finland, not used elsewhere in Scandinavia.

Finnish pedestrian crossings: 5 white stripes.

Sweden/Norway crossings: 4 stripes. Norway occasionally uses 5 stripes (rare).

Traffic signposts in Finland often feature blue and white stripes.

Sweden uses a similar pattern. Estonia shares the colors but with wider, looser markings.

Finnish directional signs: Blue background with white text/border and arrows.

Finnish street signs: Names usually end with "tie" (road) or "katu" (street), typically white background with black text.

Finnish utility poles:

  • Mostly wooden, simple design
  • Various poletop styles
  • Often include street lights
